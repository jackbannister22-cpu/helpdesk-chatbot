Christo: First question is about running a first Facebook ad campaign, specifically using a giveaway to grow a following. The proposed outline is to offer a 5-star Newcastle getaway: a luxurious night for two at a high-end hotel, plus dinner at a notable restaurant. The entry requirements are to follow the business, share the post, with entries closing and a winner announced 1st of August. 

The big issue with this is that Facebook's policy does not allow “follow and share” as entry requirements anymore. Even though you might see others do it, Facebook will not approve the ad—it's a consistent policy. What you *can* do is encourage people to tag friends in the comments. For instance: "Tag three friends below—an office manager, someone who could use a clean, your clean-freak friend, whoever fits.” This tagging style is acceptable on Facebook and can increase exposure.

Client: Do I offer a free clean to everyone who tags or just as a prize?

Christo: Just as a prize for a draw. You don’t have to give everyone a free clean—simply tag to go in the draw.

Client: I approached the hotel and restaurant, who are giving me a free night’s accommodation for the giveaway. They love me because I clean their common areas.

Christo: The challenge is, with a big prize like this, people might focus more on the hotel and dinner, not your actual business. There's a risk they just want the prize, not your service. If you make the prize in-house—like a free cleaning job—you’re more likely to attract people who are actual potential clients or leads.

Participant: I’ve seen with other businesses that if the giveaway is something like a hotel, people focus on the hotel, not the business running the giveaway. Not backed up by data, but just a potential risk.

Client: Should I pivot and instead promote the new pressure cleaning services? For example, give away a free driveway clean to launch that service, ask people to tag someone with a messy driveway?

Christo: Yes, that’s a better approach. Try to get people to tag people who would be leads—office managers, “messiest friend,” someone who needs their driveway cleaned. When you do these kinds of posts, film a before-and-after, make it fun, add a catchy song. It actually performs well on social platforms and acts as a great mini-promo.

Participant: You could do the giveaway by running “book a job this month for a chance to win,” or even refund a lucky customer with a free clean after they've paid—so you’re giving them their money back as a surprise.

Christo: That’s a good angle as well, and another strategy, which I heard from a retail group, is to time the campaign with end-of-financial year. For example, “get your tax return and use it to clean up your home or driveway!” Reframe the offer around people having a little extra money from tax time—people connect with these ideas.

Another engagement strategy: “Tag your messiest friend who clearly needs a driveway clean.” People love a bit of banter. Or, prompt an email opt-in for your newsletter as an entry mechanic: “Enter your email for a chance to win.” If you do this, run a proper lead campaign that drives people to your website's opt-in form. These leads become assets in your mailing list.

Client: That’s good. I’ve been reading about building my email asset. So this would get me emails and set up my list, even if the newsletter doesn’t exist yet.

Christo: Exactly. It will nudge you to get it going, and when people are in your database, you can send your monthly tips or offers. But keep in mind—Facebook area targeting helps too: set radius or region so your entries are actually in your service zone, like Newcastle only if that’s your patch.

For your ad creative, keep it natural and organic. A casual video of you talking, not over-produced—invite people to tag friends or explain the giveaway. If it looks like an ad, people just scroll past it. You want the authenticity to fit the platform.

Client: I have four kids, so I can easily find a dirty carpet to demo a before-and-after!

Christo: Perfect! Use what you’ve got—show a lived-in setup; it’s relatable and practical.

Bring your draft ads to the online community or next advisory board. Start with a small budget (even $5 a day), see results, and scale if it’s working.

Kayla: A couple of general advertising queries:

1. If you post a video to Instagram that looks like a promo/advert, rather than an organic/fun post, does Instagram limit the reach algorithmically?

Christo: Yes. Instagram and Facebook watch for content that seems promotional. If your post reads or looks like an ad, they restrict its organic reach—they want you to pay to promote actual ads. To maximize organic reach, keep captions conversational and natural ("Check out this crazy wave!") instead of bullet points and offers.

Kayla: If you post the same content multiple times, does the platform treat it as spam?

Christo: Yes, posting the same thing twice can dilute reach because the engagement is split and the algorithm flags it as spammy. Collaborate posts instead of reposting.

2. For Google AdWords, is it smartest to test the Australian market before trying internationally? Is it too expensive to go global straight away?

Christo: Definitely start in Australia. You get decent reach on a local budget and can refine your ads before scaling internationally. When you do expand, be selective—pick countries where there’s a strong market for your service, not just “anywhere.” You can also hyper-target by Australian state if you know some areas are more relevant.
