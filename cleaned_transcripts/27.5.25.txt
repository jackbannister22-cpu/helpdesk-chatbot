On magic numbers: you’re going to start measuring some metrics, not too many. For example, measure weekly website traffic (using Google Analytics). Your targets might be a guess at first, e.g., “I want 100 visitors per week.” The actual numbers come from your analytics.

Google Analytics is free. If tech isn’t your area, outsource setup to a tech freelancer (I use someone overseas for small tasks). For anything tech, don’t waste hours—pay a pro. For other things like Meta ads and website pixels, same logic: get someone savvy.

Look for trends in your numbers. You could use the traffic light feature: set ranges that are green/amber/red for your targets. Keep it simple if you want—just tracking numbers is valuable.

Participant: What is an average/target for website traffic?

Christo: There's no universal average. More traffic isn’t always better if it’s the wrong audience. The main thing: is it growing and is it the right people (local, relevant audience)? Also track leads (inquiries per week), conversion rate (how many inquiries book in), average spend, and total weekly revenue.

Participant: Google Business is useful and free for traffic/keywords/reviews.

Christo: Great tip—Google My Business is powerful for local businesses, and yes, it gives you search stats, impressions, popular keywords, etc.

Lead tracking: track the number of leads for each of your campaigns. Start using the sheet for your traffic column and magic numbers.

Frequency: if you’re part-time, monthly metrics may suffice; if full-time, do it weekly. Book in a regular meeting with yourself (and/or your leadership team) to review numbers—this is critical for growth. What you measure grows.
