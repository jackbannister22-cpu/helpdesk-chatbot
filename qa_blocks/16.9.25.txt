Question: Why is it important to present work-in-progress materials (like a flyer) to an advisory board, even if they're not yet perfect?
Answer: Presenting unfinished materials, even if they're rough or incomplete, is vital because it gets your ideas out of your head and into reality, making progress rather than letting perfectionism stall you. The advisory board provides feedback, helps you refine the work, and prevents you from getting stuck trying to get it "right" before taking action. As Christo says, advisory boards exist to help you get things done rather than perfect from the start.
Key Insight: Progress is more important than perfection; sharing early drafts with peers accelerates clarity and practical action.

Question: How should you approach writing headlines for a marketing flyer in the mental health/health industry?
Answer: The headline on your flyer is the most valuable real estate—David Ogilvy said that 80 cents of every marketing dollar should be spent on the headline, as it’s key to grabbing attention. Assume your reader knows less than you do and avoid jargon unless you’re certain your target audience is familiar with the terms. The headline should intrigue the right person enough to keep reading. For clinical fields, consider if the term (e.g., "DBT") is widely recognized by your audience or if you need to spell out and clarify, like "Dialectical Behavioral Therapy."
Key Insight: Headlines are your main hook; make them clear, targeted, and accessible to your specific audience.

Question: Should specialized program details (like "DBT" or "adults only") be made explicit on marketing materials?
Answer: Yes, always clarify who your program is for and what it covers. If your offering is only for adults, specify this. If your audience might not recognize technical terms, use language that speaks to their situation or pain points (“struggling with overwhelming emotions”) and clarify any acronyms. This prevents potential clients from being confused or feeling rejected if they’re not the right demographic.
Key Insight: Be explicit and audience-specific in your materials to remove barriers and encourage the right people to engage.

Question: How can you build social proof and credibility in health service marketing without breaching professional codes?
Answer: If you can’t use testimonials due to professional guidelines, use other credible forms of social proof: state how many years you’ve been in practice, total years of collective team experience, business or industry awards, media mentions, memberships, or accreditations. Logos or factual longevity statements (“trusted provider in the community for over 15 years”) work well. Avoid calling yourself a "guru" or anything not factually supportable.
Key Insight: Ethical statements of experience, longevity, and legitimate associations can effectively build trust without crossing regulatory lines.

Question: How should you handle communicating program pricing and payment options for therapy services?
Answer: Make payment options and eligibility (e.g., Medicare, NDIS) as clear and accessible as possible—especially since potential clients may be unwell or financially concerned. Highlight available support, such as “Scan Me” links for more details, and avoid overwhelming people with complexity. If health funds aren’t currently an option but may be in future, indicate that transparently.
Key Insight: Clear, simple communication about costs and accessible options reassures clients and removes a primary barrier to engagement.

Question: How do you address the intimidating nature of long-term commitment (like a 6-month program) in mental health services marketing?
Answer: Frame the program in a supportive and stepwise way. Emphasize personalization and ongoing support (“all-encompassing program,” “step-by-step,” “we help you all the way through”), and highlight any individualized/collaborative aspects. Clarify that while the structure is set, content is tailored, and client-therapist collaboration is central. This personalization can reduce the perceived burden and increase buy-in.
Key Insight: Personalization and support messaging make long programs less daunting and more approachable for clients.

Question: What are recommended tools and workflows for editing and producing online course videos as a beginner?
Answer: For do-it-yourself video editing, Canva is great for beginners and already familiar to many virtual assistants; it provides drag-and-drop features, templates, and stock media. Capwing is another user-friendly video editor with a similar feel. Use a workflow in which your assistant edits, you review, and you iterate back and forth for feedback. If your current VA lacks capacity, platforms like Upwork can connect you with external editors, but leveraging your existing team’s familiarity with your brand is usually faster and cheaper.
Key Insight: Use simple, familiar tools like Canva, and maximize workflow efficiency by working with people who know your branding.

Question: What’s the best approach to creating and pitching a paid workshop at the end of a free webinar?
Answer: When transitioning from a free webinar to selling a paid workshop, first set your dates for the workshops (ideally 2–4 weeks out for planning). Adjust the flyer or promo material to reflect the change from a free to a paid offering. Start talking about the upcoming offer with 10 minutes left in the webinar, not after you’ve closed the session. Clearly explain what will be covered, who it’s for, where and when, and remove uncertainty. Give a very clear, actionable call to action (“click this link now,” or “put your details in the chat”). Use urgency and incentives—offer a “today only” price or explain limited availability to encourage immediate action.
Key Insight: Pitch inside the webinar while you have attention, make details clear, and use urgency and incentives to drive sign-ups.

Question: How should you price entry-level training workshops to balance credibility and momentum?
Answer: Pricing too low (e.g., $85 for a 3-hour workshop) can undermine perceived value and professionalism, especially in professional development. Do a “stomach test” and see what feels fair for the skills offered ($125–$197 is often more appropriate). You can offer an “introductory” or “today only” special price to build momentum but avoid devaluing your brand. Monitor sales volume at different prices and refine accordingly, but don’t go so low that people doubt your quality.
Key Insight: Price reflects perceived value—set rates that signal quality while providing occasional timelimited specials to boost uptake.

Question: How important is having a beautiful website compared to delivering value in professional training and workshops?
Answer: Having an impressive website is less important than delivering real, transformative value in your workshop or service. Clients will overlook mediocre web presence if they receive outcomes worth their investment. Focus on the experience and skills you deliver, and let your pricing and reputation reflect your impact, not just your digital look.
Key Insight: Value and outcomes are more important than marketing polish; substance wins over style in service-based businesses.
