Question:  
What is the best way to structure a Facebook giveaway ad campaign to grow business following, given Facebook’s rules?

Answer:  
Facebook no longer allows “follow and share” as entry requirements for giveaways. Although you may see others using it, Facebook will not approve your ad with these criteria. The compliant and effective method is to encourage people to tag friends in the comments (e.g., "Tag three friends below—your office manager, clean-freak friend, or anyone who could use our service"). This boosts reach and exposure without violating policy.

Key Insight:  
Stay up-to-date with Facebook’s promotional policy; using tagging as an entry mechanic is both effective and compliant, while “follow and share to enter” will get your ad disapproved.

Question:  
Should the giveaway prize focus on something external (like a hotel stay) or your own service?

Answer:  
Offering a big external prize, such as a hotel night and restaurant dinner, attracts entrants more interested in the prize than your business. If you make the giveaway your own service (like a free clean), you’re more likely to attract actual prospective clients or leads.

Key Insight:  
Align your prize with your service—target people who are likely to be future customers, not just anyone chasing a freebie.

Question:  
How can you tailor a giveaway to promote a new service, such as pressure cleaning?

Answer:  
Make the prize relate to the new service (e.g., “free driveway clean”). Ask entrants to tag someone who could use the service—this attracts real leads. Use a before-and-after demo video, make it engaging, and use a catchy song for social platforms.

Key Insight:  
Tightly match your giveaway to the service you want to promote; this way, entrants are actual prospects. Fun, relatable video content performs well.

Question:  
What alternative giveaway mechanics can deepen customer engagement and generate leads?

Answer:  
Aside from tagging friends, you can run campaigns like “Book a job this month for a chance to win” or refund the cost of a clean to a lucky customer post-payment. Prompting email opt-ins (“enter your email for a chance to win”) helps build a mailing list asset—just ensure to use a proper website opt-in form.

Key Insight:  
Leverage your giveaway to build owned assets (like an email list), and consider creative mechanics that reward actual buyers, not just entrants.

Question:  
How important is targeting your giveaway geographically and keeping ad creatives authentic?

Answer:  
Use Facebook’s area targeting to ensure entrants are from your service area. For creative, keep videos casual and authentic (e.g., using your own home and family for demos), as ads that look too polished or “ad-like” reduce engagement.

Key Insight:  
Authenticity beats polish on social platforms, and geo-targeting ensures you’re reaching leads in your actual market.

Question:  
How should you approach new logo and brand development for a health-focused home design business?

Answer:  
“Home Design” is broad and can cause confusion. Pair it with a clear, literal tagline that spells out your core offering (“Reducing mold. Reducing toxins. Enhancing airflow”), so prospects instantly “get” what you do. The logo’s role is secondary to a clear message.

Key Insight:  
Go literal and benefit-based with your tagline so that people remember the main value you offer—clarity beats creativity in crowded spaces.

Question:  
How can a brand name or tagline become more niche and effective?

Answer:  
If the offering is broad, consider reworking the tagline or even the name for specificity (“Healthy Home Design” or “Hazard Reduction Design”) and clarity. Use taglines that quickly signal your specialty (“Healthy nursery setups for new parents”).

Key Insight:  
The clearer and more benefit-driven your brand and tagline, the more likely you attract the right market—and niche offers (like healthy nurseries) give you a definable entry point.

Question:  
What makes an effective logo for a small business, considering usage across platforms?

Answer:  
Logos must be legible at different sizes. Have multiple versions—icon, landscape, and stacked. Avoid complex fonts that won’t scale well. Clarity and flexibility are more important than ornament.

Key Insight:  
Design for versatility and legibility over style; your logo needs to work everywhere from website favicons to merch.

Question:  
How do you approach offering a broad service spectrum without getting lost in complexity?

Answer:  
Don’t try to communicate everything at once. Present three clear offerings or major problems you solve. Use the most immediate need as the entry point, then introduce other services after clients connect with you.

Key Insight:  
Simplify your offers—clarity and specificity draw people in, while breadth and complexity can wait for later stages in the customer relationship.

Question:  
Does posting video ads on Instagram that look promotional reduce organic reach?

Answer:  
Yes. Instagram (and Facebook) limit organic reach for posts that look or read like an ad. Posts that are conversational, casual, and story-like get more traction; save promotional style for actual paid ads.

Key Insight:  
Organic reach favors natural, in-the-moment posts—keep promotions subtle unless you’re putting spend behind them.

---

Question:  
If you post identical content multiple times to Instagram, will it impact reach?

Answer:  
Yes. Posting the same content more than once divides engagement and trains the algorithm to see it as spammy, limiting its reach. Collaborate or repurpose content instead of reposting identically.

Key Insight:  
Variety and collaboration perform better than repetition—avoid duplicating organic posts.

Question:  
Should you test Google Ads in Australia first or go international straight away?

Answer:  
Start in your local market (e.g., Australia) to maximize budget and refine your messaging and targeting. Only expand internationally once you have learnings and can see where else your service might fit well.

Key Insight:  
Test and optimize campaigns locally before scaling—select international markets carefully rather than going broad immediately.

Question:  
What is the ongoing process for improving brand messaging and visual identity?

Answer:  
Design and brand messaging are iterative. Test your messaging and visuals, gather feedback, and refine. Have adaptable logo variants for different placements. Evolve both the look and proposition as you grow in clarity.

Key Insight:  
Expect your brand and messaging to evolve—iteration, feedback, and adaptation are critical for clarity and referable offers.
