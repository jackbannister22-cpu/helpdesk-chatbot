Question: When creating Facebook ads, is it better to use videos or still images for visuals?
Answer: Both videos and stills can work well for Facebook ads, as long as the content is engaging and fits the platform. Stills that look like banner ads or are overly branded tend to get ignored, but authentic, lively photos—like team selfies or fun moments—grab attention. For video, make the first 4 seconds count: tightly crop, start with something interesting, and avoid long intros or branded logos. Ultimately, choose visuals that match social media’s preference for lifestyle and personal content.
Key Insight: Success comes not from picking one format, but from making engaging content that feels social and authentic; avoid overly polished or obviously templated visuals.

Question: How can I better organize and select my photos and videos for ads and social media?
Answer: After each trip or event, pick two or three standout photos or clips and save them in a dedicated folder for ads and social posts. This narrows down your choices when it’s time to create campaigns. For video, consider scripting or sketching ideas (ChatGPT or similar tools can help draft scripts). Use your most visually engaging content and keep a small, easily accessible library ready to go.
Key Insight: Proactively curating a shortlist of strong content will save overwhelm and make ad creation faster.

Question: How do I know which types of posts and content resonate best with my audience on social media?
Answer: Review your old posts, especially organic ones, to see what received higher engagement. Posts sharing personal stories, emotional moments, or behind-the-scenes details (like stories about favorite skippers or family connections) often perform much better than standard updates. People are attracted to authenticity, drama, and personal touches—even if it’s just quirky customs or glimpses into local life.
Key Insight: Analyze past engagement to find patterns—personal and emotionally real content tends to drive reactions more than generic updates.

Question: How can I make it easier to create and retrieve engaging content while running trips?
Answer: Make a habit of taking photos and short videos regularly and immediately saving two or three per trip into a special folder. Over time, this habit will become natural. Consider organizing content into themed “buckets” (e.g., quirky facts, food, guest comments) to make finding content easier later. Start by choosing two or three key themes.
Key Insight: Small organizational habits (selecting, saving, bucketing) prevent content overwhelm and make future posts effortless.

Question: How should I request customer reviews, and what’s the best timing and process for follow-up?
Answer: When asking for platform reviews (like Google or TripAdvisor), provide a focused prompt rather than a general “please review us.” For example, ask about their thoughts on the destination and service. For testimonials to use on your own website or collateral, ask these four questions: (1) Did you have concerns before booking? (2) Why did you choose us? (3) What did you love most? (4) Who would you recommend us to? Request reviews in-person at the end of the experience and follow up immediately with an accessible link or QR code. Audio-only feedback or text is an option for those uncomfortable on camera.
Key Insight: Direct, timely requests using specific prompts and easy mechanisms (links/QR codes) dramatically increase follow-through.

Question: Why do most guests say they’ll leave a review but don’t follow through, and how can I improve my conversion rate?
Answer: Many people simply forget or get distracted after leaving. The best approach is to prompt them for a review at the optimal moment—right as the experience wraps up—and provide them an immediate, easy way to take action (e.g., handing a QR code, texting a link, or asking in person). Using WhatsApp or another group chat for your trips makes real-time prompting convenient.
Key Insight: Success hinges on asking at the right emotional moment and offering instant, low-friction ways to respond.

Question: What wording and format work best for a service business purpose statement, especially when moving from a niche (like podiatry) to a broader multidisciplinary clinic?
Answer: Short, emotive, and brand-aligned statements are most effective. Examples offered were “Helping you move better, feel better, and live better...” with the suggestion to swap in a more emotional phrase like “live with a smile.” Removing generic openers like “helping you” makes the purpose statement punchier. Ensuring the wording fits the broader focus of the clinic is essential—as is possibly re-evaluating the clinic’s name if it’s too niche.
Key Insight: Clear, emotionally resonant language aligned with the intended scope gives credibility and energy to your brand purpose.

Question: What are best practices for recruiting and onboarding virtual assistants (VAs), particularly from abroad?
Answer: Use reputable platforms when recruiting, especially from countries like the Philippines. Screen applicants using practical “test tasks” that reflect typical responsibilities (like handling emails, creating social posts, or dealing with common admin scenarios). This approach tests real capabilities, especially communication and fit for your service standard.
Key Insight: Real-world scenario assessments during hiring yield team members far better matched to your processes and service style.

Question: How should I set expectations and build culture for a growing, multidisciplinary team?
Answer: Develop a culture book that documents your team’s values, communication styles, rituals, and best practices (for example, “no blame,” “support each other”). Use it as an onboarding tool and as an ongoing reference for team alignment.
Key Insight: Explicit documentation of culture creates shared understanding, strengthens team unity, and accelerates onboarding.
