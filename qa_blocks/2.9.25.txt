Question: What should be the main focus and headline of a personal website for someone building a thought leadership brand around trust, collaboration, and wisdom in leadership?

Answer: The website headline should be clear and specific to the target audience. Instead of just listing credentials (“Professor Paul Atkins, author, facilitator, researcher”), use a headline like “Empowering purpose-driven leaders to navigate complexity while maintaining humanity and effectiveness.” This instantly tells visitors what you help them with, rather than just titles. You can then use a subheading to quickly build credibility, listing roles like "author, facilitator, keynote speaker, researcher."

Key Insight: The first three seconds are crucial—make your specialty and audience crystal clear immediately so visitors know they are in the right place and keep reading.

Question: How should service offerings like keynote speaking and workshops be presented on a personal branding site, especially if you lack a corporate keynote track record?

Answer: List your offerings in a “Work With [Name]” section, with subheadings like “Keynote Speaking,” “Workshops and Training,” and “Books.” Write brief, clear summaries under each so visitors quickly see how they can engage with you. Even if you don’t have a long corporate keynote history, you can still list keynote speaking as a service. Also, include a “Who [Name] Works With” area, naming the types of clients (leaders, organizations, groups) to help ideal clients self-identify.

Key Insight: Make it easy for event planners or clients to scan and see your main offerings, even if your corporate experience is limited—clarity and relevance outweigh an extensive track record.

Question: How should newsletter or Substack signups be described on a personal website?

Answer: Replace simple logos or vague prompts with clear benefits. For example: “Get monthly leadership insights direct to your inbox” or “Join my newsletter for inspiration and updates.” Assume people don’t know what Substack is—explain why they should subscribe and what’s in it for them.

Key Insight: Spell out the value plainly; never assume visitors understand platforms or how your content helps them.

Question: What visual and messaging tweaks strengthen a personal brand website?

Answer: Use an additional photo of yourself to add personality and trust. Make sure small print is legible and don’t fade out important information, especially details of key offerings or programs. Bring crucial sections like “current work” up front, and avoid bland section titles like “About.” Instead, use “Why Work With Paul” or a headline describing what you do.

Key Insight: Improve both the look and clarity of messaging, making it easy to skim and inviting for visitors to quickly understand your value.

Question: What are recommended platforms for building landing pages and managing email automations for small businesses?

Answer: Many CRMs include landing page functionality. Ontraport is recommended for small businesses since it covers emails, automations, payments, etc., and is responsive to support queries. If running a big e-commerce store, Shopify is leading; for simple landing pages with payment, Ontraport is suitable. Klaviyo is popular with e-commerce stores and integrates well with Shopify. Both are good choices for starting out with email capture and lead magnets—choose one and use it unless you hit clear limitations.

Key Insight: Don’t overanalyze platform choices—select a good fit, set it up for core needs, and only switch if the platform blocks your progress.

Question: How can non-technical business owners get help setting up landing pages or CRM integrations?

Answer: Hire through platforms like Upwork for specialist help with Ontraport, Klaviyo, etc. Ask your service provider for a video walkthrough so you can make simple updates yourself. Upwork often has higher-end talent than Fiverr/Freelancer. Filter by location or budget for the right fit.

Key Insight: Outsource the tech setup but always request a guide or video so you maintain control for future tweaks.

Question: What’s the best way to build an in-store customer mailing list when customers are rushed or resistant?

Answer: Offer a clear incentive for signing up live—like “join our subscriber list for exclusive offers,” “go in the draw to win,” or simply “would you like your receipt emailed to you?” Focus on collecting details directly at checkout rather than handing out pamphlets, which are often ignored.

Key Insight: Capture data in the moment with a tangible benefit, not indirect asks or materials that are easy to discard.

Question: How do you interpret Google Analytics reports for small businesses?

Answer: In page reports, “checkout” means a user has reached the checkout page; a thank you or order confirmation page indicates a sale. “Direct” in acquisition reports means traffic where Google can’t attribute the source (typed URL or untracked link). Demographic missing data often means you don’t have enough traffic—data depth increases with higher site visits.

Key Insight: Learn to interpret basic Google Analytics terms—clarity improves as traffic grows, so focus on gradual improvement and regular checks.

Question: What’s the process for tracking and improving “magic numbers” or KPIs on a weekly basis?

Answer: Track metrics from the start of the financial year, monitoring weekly for continual improvement rather than rigid targets. Track averages, and target incremental improvement (“green” means raise the bar; “red,” lower to maintain motivation). Request and accepted quotes can be tracked alongside traffic and ad spends to link activities to outcomes.

Key Insight: Tracking weekly KPIs highlights bottlenecks and opportunities for growth; over time, patterns will help you improve both efficiency and results.

Question: How do you track the impact of specific advertising activities (radio, Facebook ads) on weekly results?

Answer: Record ad spend or activity in a weekly “traffic” section, noting correlation with results like leads or conversions. This lets you connect marketing actions to changes in performance over time.

Key Insight: Connect activity (what you do) to outcome (what you achieve) for better marketing decisions.

Question: What’s the best way to track pageviews by product category in Google Analytics, and can events be set up to sum all subpages?

Answer: Initially, Google Analytics can sum category and subpage traffic. For more detail, set up Events to group or track specific actions on grouped category pages. For ad campaigns targeting categories, track conversions and link them to your key performance metrics for reporting.

Key Insight: Use basic tracking first, upgrade to custom Events as needs become clearer.

Question: What is the most effective messaging for event flyers and what should be the focus in headlines and benefits?

Answer: Start with a clear benefit or the problem you solve, not just the event name. For example: “Wish you could communicate better under pressure?” or “Struggling with team conflict?” Follow with your unique method or solution (“discover neuroaffirming ways…”). Add social proof, like testimonials or logos, and avoid jargon. Keep promotional details simple—focus on key takeaways rather than deep frameworks.

Key Insight: Put the main benefit and hook upfront; keep language accessible and let deeper insights unfold during the event itself.

Question: How should events be promoted for best reach?

Answer: Use every channel available—your existing networks, social media, email, business networking, and especially relevant Facebook or LinkedIn groups. Consider ads for broader reach and partnerships with aligned organizations.

Key Insight: Promotion is best approached as a multi-channel effort—don’t limit yourself to one or two sources.

Question: Should you promote multiple events together or separately?

Answer: It depends on the relevance and overlap of audiences, but clarity and focus are key for each event. Flyers should be clear and differentiated; make updates based on feedback and resubmit for further input if needed.

Key Insight: Tailor communications to the audience and event purpose—group if highly related, separate if focus or benefits differ.
