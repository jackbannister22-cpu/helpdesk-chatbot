Question: How should Amy name, structure, and price her online couples counseling program to maximize its appeal and reach?
Answer: Christo and Franziska suggest Amy uses a relatable and emotionally resonant name, possibly adding a subtitle with keywords like "connection, conflict, and communication" to speak directly to what couples are seeking. They recommend testing price points through a free masterclass/webinar, where valuable content is shared and the program is offered at a special discount (e.g., public price $997, but $497 for webinar attendees). Module delivery should be “five modules over five weeks” with reminders and lifetime access. The price should reflect the program's real value, not be the cheapest, and messaging should compare the cost to the high cost of relationship breakdown (e.g. "Is your relationship worth $500?").
Key Insight: Test both messaging and price in real-time (e.g., via a masterclass) and position the program and its value in emotionally compelling, real-world terms that resonate with the target audience.

Question: How can Amy make her program and module descriptions more enticing and emotionally resonant?
Answer: Christo and Franziska advise using emotional, real-life scenarios rather than clinical language. They suggest, for example, reframing modules from "greater connection" to “from not feeling seen or heard to real connection.” Christo recommends using ChatGPT to make wording more relatable, spiced up, and emotionally impactful—eliciting a "Yes, I feel that" response.
Key Insight: Use the language and real-life feelings of your audience in your messaging to make your course irresistible, moving from generic to deeply relatable communication.

Question: What visual frameworks or conceptual models help clients understand their relationship journey and the value of Amy’s program?

Question: How can Amy improve the clarity and effectiveness of her website homepage and landing pages?
Answer: Focus the homepage on practical, accessible support and clarify who it’s for. Avoid overly philosophical or generic language (like “we’re all connected” without context). Instead, use a summary that says exactly what Amy offers and to whom, then direct with a single, clear call-to-action (like "learn more about our framework"). Franziska also suggests moving extra options down and tightening headers for clarity.
Key Insight: Homepage and key landing pages should quickly and clearly communicate the core service and audience, using direct and specific language, with a single, prominent call to action.

Question: How should testimonials and graphic design elements be handled on Amy’s website?
Answer: Testimonials should be used sparingly and strategically—focus them on the homepage and program pages rather than every page. Design elements should be consistent, and if certain frameworks or sections aren’t ready (such as the 5Rs), it’s better to omit them until complete. Franziska also recommends using design resources like Canva templates for flyers.
Key Insight: Use testimonials and design elements intentionally to reinforce credibility and brand consistency without overwhelming or distracting from the key message.

Question: What are the key components of an effective retail lead magnet and email sequence for a store like Jody’s?
Answer: Christo and Franziska recommend a lead magnet (e.g., 10% off) tied to an opt-in that’s offered both in-store and online. The email sequence should include a welcome email with a discount and a time-limited offer (e.g., “Expires in 30 days”), reminders for unclaimed discounts, integration of real customer testimonials via screenshots (not generic text), and clear links to products. For loyalty programs, emails should feature a simple explanation, direct benefit, and a clear join link.
Key Insight: Combine in-store and online lead capture, use real testimonials, reinforce scarcity in offers, and make loyalty programs easy to join and understand for maximum engagement.

Question: How should Emma reposition her office furniture business to reflect an expanded service offering on the landing page?
Answer: Christo and Franziska suggest the landing page introduces both product (“buy office furniture”) and service (“get an office fit-out”), using simple options for the customer: shopping or booking a free consultation/measure and quote. Messaging should emphasize their position as a premium provider with commercial-grade, warrantied products and highlight their planning and fit-out expertise over “project management,” which might be less clear.
Key Insight: A clear landing page structure with two user paths (buy or full service), paired with strong differentiation as a premium, full-solution provider, is key for standing out in a crowded market.

Question: What makes an effective and eye-catching poster for Kyla’s surf school/gym app promotion?
Answer: Simple, impactful design is critical for posters—use a strong image, a concise and aspirational headline (e.g., “Improve your surfing skills by 1% every session”), and a subheadline with practical benefits or pricing (e.g., “Try free for 7 days, then less than 30 cents a day”). Avoid overcrowding the poster with details; instead, direct viewers to a place for more information (“Learn the techniques that will serve you”).
Key Insight: For posters, less is more—focus on visual appeal, aspirational messaging, and a clear call to action, using feedback and iteration to hone what works.

Question: What overall mindset and approach should business owners adopt as they develop their messaging, offers, and materials?
Answer: Christo and Franziska encourage continuous iteration—test messages, prices, and creative assets with real audiences, refine based on feedback, focus on clarity over cleverness, and make decisions grounded in real customer experience and data, not guesswork.
Key Insight: Launch with what you have, gather data and feedback, and keep refining until your offer and message strongly resonate with your audience.
